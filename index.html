Project Description:

Overview:

We propose to create an application that will estimate the user's risk of crime, either as an individual or as a business. This application will take the form of a questionnaire, which will ask the user increasingly specific questions and return increasingly accurate assessments of risk. What we really hope to accomplish through this app is to give individuals and businesses a better understanding of their risk of being a victim of a crime, and what crimes they are subject to. We believe that with an informed understanding, people can make better decisions about what level of risk exposure they are comfortable with, and at what point they should take extra steps to reduce that risk. The scope of our project is such that, with continued development, we intend to provide users with customised tips based on criminological theory and their own input about how they can mitigate their risk.  

Our application will not be a silver bullet to forever eliminate the fear of crime, we simply aim to give people an improved understanding of their own risk and the steps they can take to reduce it. The outcomes we are aiming for are focused on increasing knowledge and awareness of risk of crime, and thus better equipping them with the knowledge of what they should focus their risk reduction efforts on and how they can reduce risk. In addition, by utilizing real statistics, we are aiming to help increase confidence for businesses to open in areas that are popularly seen as being dangerous, but with no higher risk than anywhere else. We think that this program could make it more accessible for businesses to open in the neighborhoods that would genuinely benefit from investment, without the fear of crime as a barrier. Finally, when we reach the stage that we can collect our own reliable data on crime rates and demographics, we hope to be a resource for crime prevention efforts on a larger scale, by working with local, state, and federal governments.

Our motivation to create this program comes from our interest in crime, not just within our group, but as a society at large. Crime is an enormous part of our collective consciousness, it is all over the news every day, in our movies, books, tv shows, podcasts, and in our music. This saturation with crime means that it is always on people's minds. We think, and it has been well documented, that crime is not as serious a problem as it has become in our public consciousness (Callanan, 2005) (Lee and Mythen, 2017). We want to help people understand their risk based on statistics and data, not on the perception they get from the news and social media. This application is interesting to use because it is a simple premise that could profoundly change the way society thinks about their relationship to crime. This program may be a working example to a future employer that we have been effective working as a team, be able to set a solid structure for what we want to accomplish, and that we are passionate about using technology to help people better understand their surroundings. 

There are similar programs and web apps that have been operating for some time. Websites such as NeighbourhoodScout.com and PerthCrimeMap.com both show statistics for crimes over a map view based on suburb. Government bodies like the ABS (Australian Bureau of Statistics) and Crime Statistics Agency in Victoria both keep detailed statistics on the rate, type, and location of crime. What our proposed application will do differently is make the data more specific than the website crime maps, and more accessible than the government agencies. The interactive questionnaire nature of our app, where the user decides whether to progress to the next question or exit the program, means that our application can be as general or as specific as the user wants it to be. There are also a host of public resources available to help people reduce their risk of crime, some of these are also based on criminological statistics. However, in our reading, none are customised to the reader or user, and none are packaged with a risk assessment tool. Our application would therefore be the first on the market to bridge the gap between the general assessments of crime map websites, the near inaccessibility of government statistics, and the general advice of online government and insurance advice on reducing risk. Further, our intention to create the program as an application means that it will be the first of its kind available outside of a web browser on a phone. Finally, while there are proposals to use machine learning to predict crime, this functionality is restricted to use in policing, and is focused on tracking hotspots, rather than being used to predict the likelihood of crime victimization for a person (Shah, Bhagat and Shah, 2021) (Kshatri et al., 2021).

Aims

“Develop a questionnaire program to determine the user’s risk of crime”

Our aim is to calculate the user’s actual risk of being a victim of a crime. Through developing a quiz to ask the user questions and using those responses we aim to calculate the actual risk as a percentage of the user being a victim of a crime. The choice to program our application in the form of a questionnaire is intended to make it more accessible to the widest variety of users. We will accomplish this using crime statistics stored as variables. Our program will calculate the risk based on data such as: the demographic they are in, the suburb they live in, whether they have been a victim of a crime recently, etc. We believe this will reduce crime through education as our quiz will provide information on strategies to avoid the crimes that the program determines they are most at risk of. We also hope that with a better understanding of their actual risk, people can make more informed decisions about their risk. 

“Create a database of crime information” 

These crimes may include:

  • Child Protection
  • Counterfeit Currency
  • Cyber Crime
  • Drug Crime
  • Environmental Crime
  • Family Law
  • Terrorism
  • Fraud
  • Human Trafficking
  • Intellectual property crime
  • People Smuggling

Proceeds of Crime (AFP (Australian Federal Police), 2021) 

As we can see from the above list, there are still many sub-categories to explore, and this is not something researched or expected that a reasonable person would do. We will use crime statistics to fill out most of this data and the answers people provide in the quiz will correlate on this information. 

The source of the data is also crucial. Data that relies only on crimes that people have been convicted for, or only on crimes that have been reported to the police will be subject to the phenomenon of unreported crime. This means that, given that most of the crime that occurs is not reported to the police (Crime Victimisation, Australia, 2019-20 financial year, 2021), all data needs to be based on victim surveys. As the crime statistics collected by the ABS (Australian Bureau of Statistics) are based on victim surveys, this data will be used to populate the database (Crime Victimisation, Australia methodology, 2019-20 financial year, 2021).  

“Create a database of questions and answers”

 Databases are used to store application information for use and a database management system such as oracle will be used to control storage, deletion, retrieval, and security of our data. A data store is essential as the quiz will not function without data (Ambysoft, 2021).  Questions will be based on the factors that crime statistics collect, and later in the development of the program we intend to collect and use our own statistics so that we can be more specific with our risk reduction advice. We intend to structure the questionnaire to give the user their risk score after each question, with the ability to finish answering questions at any point. This will ensure that the program is as detailed or as specific as the user requires.  

“Code the quiz to be user friendly” 

In this step we want the user interface to enable users to easily fill out and receive results from our quiz. This stage will be achieved through testing, which is important as people will not use the quiz if it is confusing or uncooperative. We intend to release this program as an app on both Android and IOS, as we believe that this approach will make the app most widely available. The coding for the backend will be done in Python, with the user interface being constructed with proto.io, and the database hosted with AWS. Python is a general use programming language which two members of our team are familiar with, further, its integration with proto.io makes it well suited to this program. We have chosen proto.io for the creation of the user interface because of its synergy with the Python programming language and its ability to deploy using native elements on both Android and IOS devices (Keith-Magee, 2021). Finally, we have chosen AWS to host the database chiefly because of its cheap cost compared to hosting on other cloud services such as Oracle.  

“Create a Machine Learning Algorithm to Predict Crime”

This goal is to create a machine learning algorithm that we will train using the data we propose to collect through our own victim surveys. We intend to use a linear regression model to train this algorithm as this has been used in a comparable way to predict crime described in Crime Forecasting: A Machine Learning and Computer Vision Approach, using linear regression we will be able to predict the likelihood of victimisation by comparing the user's inputs to our database (Shah, Bhagat and Shah, 2021). We will measure the accuracy of this algorithm by using a confusion matrix, obviously aiming for as high an accuracy as we can get. We also intend to display the accuracy to the user when displaying their risk score.  

“Create the sign-in feature” 

This goal is to build an option for users to create an account to save their answers. We think this is a necessary feature as it will make using the app much more accessible if the user does not have to go through the entire process of completing the survey in its entirety every time, they want a risk assessment. We think this feature will also be helpful in allowing the user to view their updated score as we improve the accuracy of our machine learning feature or add new data with further victim surveys.

Plans

We are aiming to create an application that will run on both Android and IOS. This application is intended to take the form of a questionnaire. This questionnaire will have two tracks, one that will ask the user questions about their personal circumstances and the other that will ask questions about the circumstances of their household or business. Based on the users answers to these questions the program will put the user in a category and calculate their risk of crime. This calculation will initially be based on crime statistics collected by government agencies like the Australian Bureau of Statistics and the Crime Statistics Agency of Victoria and stored in a database that we will create and maintain. When we have seen sufficient uptake in the use of our program, we intend to run our own victim surveys to collect our own data.  

Collection of Data:

As mentioned in the plans for the project section above, we initially intend to build our database using the statistics collected by the ABS and the CSA (Child Support Agency) in Victoria. We have chosen these government bodies because of the methodology of their data collection. One of the earliest concepts that criminology students are taught is the problem of relying on statistics that come from the police or from courts. Both sources are subject to a host of factors that degrade the reliability of their data, the most significant of these is the phenomenon of unreported crime. The article Neighborhood Characteristics and the Reporting of Crime asserts that the amount of crime reported to the police is only about one third (Goudriaan, Wittebrood and Nieuwbeerta, 2005). Of the crimes that is reported to the police, these statistics can also be unreliable. Eterno, Verma, and Silverman describe how police can manipulate statistics collection usually due to pressure from management to keep consistently reducing the numbers (Eterno, Verma and Silverman, 2014). For these reasons we are building the database for our program with only statistics collected through victim surveys, and with a statistically significant sample size. The use of victim surveys means that we can avoid the problems affecting the collection of data that police and court statistics are subject to.  

As part of the scope of our program, we intend to collect our own data by conducting our own victim survey as part of the program. We intend to offer users of our program the choice of improving our data and the recommendations that our application can offer. One of the major problems identified with the National Crime Victimization Survey conducted by the US Bureau of Justice is its lack of privacy, which means that incidences of rape and sexual assault remain underreported (House, Kalsbeek and Kruttschnitt, 2014). To ensure that we get the maximum response to our survey and the best data, we intend to run the survey with as much of a focus on privacy and user anonymity as possible when questions about demographic and location data is requested. We hope that these measures ensure that respondents feel comfortable answering.

Iterations

First Iteration

Our first iteration of a plan for this program was vague. The initial idea was for the program to be programmed entirely in Python and run in the Python shell without a proper user interface but still taking the form of a questionnaire, however, due to feedback from A2, we noted that a shift away from this method was required. In the initial idea for the program, the risks assessments that the user would see would be stored as variables in Python, not in a database. The initial intent for the program was not so much to help reduce crime, but more to allow the user to make sense of the crime statistics available from the ABS. The intent was also to make the general, vague statistics that the user can look at on the ABS website and make them more applicable to the average user. From this iteration we kept the general idea of the program, we still intend for it to be a questionnaire, be programmed in Python, and make use of ABS statistics.  

Second Iteration: 

The second iteration of our program updated our program to include a SQL database to store our statistics and to be able to provide the user with links to crime prevention resources and information on criminological theory. We drew upon Chris’s experience in the creation of databases to help us design a skeleton for a questionnaire that would add percentage points to the user's risk score based on their answers to ten questions. Finally, we decided to create a user interface for our program using Tkinter, which would allow us to make the program more accessible to users who are unable to run it in the Python shell, update our SQL database, and make the program look and feel a lot more professional. 

Third and Current Iteration: 

The third and current iteration of our project is our most comprehensive. We have moved away from the plan to run our program on a laptop or desktop operating system using Tkinter and now plan to design the program as an application using proto.io, which will allow us to ship it as an application that will run on both IOS and Android devices. Proto.io is a suite of open-source software that will allow us to design our application, write it once, and deploy it on both systems. The shift toward a mobile application was taken to make the app more user friendly and accessible, platforms like the Play Store and the App Store on apple devices can be used to gain the widest possible reach into the market.

 An additional update to our plan focuses on the aim of our project. While initially we intended for the program to be a way of making crime statistics more accessible and relevant to users, we are now aiming to really make a difference in the lives of users. We want to help them better understand their risk and to be able to provide actionable advice on how they can make themselves, their families, and their businesses safer. While this difference may seem unimportant, it will subtly change the way our program takes shape. The chief difference that our change of goals will make is associated with the longer-term ambitions of our program. Once we can begin collecting our own data, we intend to give users much more specific advice on how to reduce risk. Our more specific questions and advice are intended to be informed by policing and crime reduction theory such as Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design (CPTED), the risks of repeat victimization, and advice for users that are experiencing intimate partner or family violence. We believe that this crime prevention advice can be a decisive factor to prevent future victimization and believe that it is worth the extra effort to program.

We have also elected to add in a feature that we found interesting in other programs that are available, a way for users to instantly search by their postcode or local government area and find the three most common crimes specific to that area. We also intend for this breakdown to further include the location of those crimes, and the most common victims. For example, if a user wants to look up the suburb of Armadale, the information the program would show is displayed as:

  1. Stealing – most common during daylight hours, most common targets: mobile phones
  2. Drug offences – most common during evening hours, most common target: N/A 
  3. Property Damage – most common during night hours, most common target: fast food restaurants 

We believe that this feature would enable users to quickly gain insight into the type of neighbourhood they may reside in, or are considering a relocation to, and to provide information regarding specifics to be aware of.  We also intend to add to our program the ability for users to sign in and save their data inputs. Maintaining our aim of giving control of the accuracy of the program to the user, we do not intend to make this feature mandatory. This feature is intended to facilitate gathering of updated risk assessments easier for users as we update our database and develop our machine learning algorithm (mentioned in the next paragraph) to be more accurate.  

The final feature we have decided to add to our program is also the most ambitious. We intend to add a machine learning algorithm to our program and train it to identify predictors of crime. We think this feature will really help us work toward our aim of reducing victimization, because unlike our earlier plan to relay statistics from past victim surveys to the user, the machine learning ability of the program will be able to make predictions regarding how likely the user is to experience crime in the future, based on their current answers. We are planning to use linear regression algorithms, similarly to what is currently being proposed by authors such as Shah, Bhagat, and Shah in their article Crime Forecasting: a Machine Learning and Computer Vision Approach. While these authors present a case for the utility of using machine learning to predict crimes to help police make more efficient use of limited resources, we propose using it to help the average person take steps to reduce their risk of crime (Shah, Bhagat and Shah, 2021). We will be testing the accuracy of our machine learning algorithm using a confusion matrix. This will allow us to be certain that the algorithm is predicting crimes.

Risks:

One of the biggest risks to the development of our project is the software we are depending on using to create the user interface, Beeware. As none of our team have any experience with this tool, or with any user interface design, we are relying on our ability to pick it up quickly and to a high standard.

Another risk to our development is the ability of our team to test the program. We have a limited amount of time to build a working copy of our program and to test it. The nature of our project as more of a long-term investment is also something that we will not be able to know if it is achieving its aim of reducing victimization for some time.

Another risk to the long-term success of our program is the quality of data required. To fully achieve our aim the program needs to be regularly updated with the latest data, and with a high enough sample size that the collected data is statistically reliable. At the stage where we are collecting our own data, either using opt-in surveys at the end of the program or as fully-fledged victim surveys, we can ensure that our data is reliable. Prior to that stage we will need to rely on the statistics collected by government agencies, which may not be as specific as we would like them to be.
